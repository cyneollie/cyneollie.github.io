Spring Break Thoughts

What’s on My Mind When the Pause Button Is Hit?
stolaf
reflection
spring
Author

Cynthia Budiyanto

Published

April 3, 2025

It is halfway through spring break. The Hill is obviously quiet and deserted; I mentioned that despite how much I hate the myriad of ways that it could be discouragingly overwhelming. This place has its magical ways to withstand the crowds, frame the chaos, gather and hold people together. I have lost count of the number of times I curse Stav during dinner time rush, wishing that they built two dining spaces like that one college - that I strangely can’t remember the name? - just across the river.

Simply put, for almost every single break where I had to stay on the Hill, I observed and told myself how this place is meant to be lively, full of people, and give you that feeling of warmth- kind of juxtaposing its famous first impression of having that mysterious… dark academia vibes owing to its architecture. Now, having read that sentence, you might think that I am over-romanticising or over-describing this place (one-year-ago-myself would probably cringe over this description), but I am starting to get the distinctive vibe this place gives off after having visited other “American colleges”. There is a fixed, set atmosphere and ambience, notoriously due to its Norwegian and Church inspired architecture style that you probably could not find anywhere else, considering its status as a small private liberal art college in the midwest (or more like, in the middle of nowhere…).

Like, it is easily distinguishable - imagine yourself being in a geoguessr game and you are suddenly being dropped at any point within St. Olaf complex - you would KNOW instantly, that you are here, on the Hill. I’m not joking - and I thought that would not be the case with other American colleges, and yes, I am biased, but you just have got to experience this place firsthand. At first, the architecture could feel oddly strange - but then you kind of realise that you could not really find this anywhere else. Other American colleges look nothing like this place, and that was my first surprise as an international student who had never visited or toured other colleges before coming here.

Coming back to that one college across the river, yes, they do have two smaller dining spaces, which are way less overwhelming and overstimulating and whatnot, but during my first time visiting there - with crazily high expectations - my jaw dropped, perplexed rather than astonished, “Is this it?”. My eyes were hovering everywhere, trying to search for corners and sights of wonders or even peculiarities that could satisfy my curiosity with this place (hint: I never did). I remember vividly when I was eating- which some Oles described the food to be better (just because of their soft-serve machine, which I did not even bother to try) - my mind replayed some familiar scenes from when I was sitting in a random shopping mall food court back home. Like, I could be dropped on this place in a geoguessr game, and I would guess, “This could be either a food court somewhere in TP or Ciputra World” (IYKYK). If there is one thing that I saw and thought “damn, St. Olaf could never”, is the fact that they have three waffle machines (we have two) - but that lasted a good two seconds and I moved on, so anyway…

But with St. Olaf’s Stav - despite how overwhelming it is - I recalled how I have always been in awe every time I stepped inside, particularly after stepping outside of the buffet area to the actual dining hall. Special mention especially when it is in the morning and during golden hour time. It is majestic - I have to acknowledge that, and my mind screams “Oh my God” every time I have to find somewhere to sit, both because (guess what?!) I could probably not find anywhere to sit since it is so crowded, but also because, oh my God! It is so grand, majestic, the huge ass windows give me reasons and hopes to live (OK, that’s kind of dramatic), and the damn tall ceilings set the bar high for a college dining hall - it is an experience of just coming in there.

Coming from someone introverted, mediocrely social and frequently wishes that we had fewer students on campus; therefore, everywhere does not always have to be packed with everyone, every time - I hate to admit this, but St. Olaf is better off with these crazy, chaotic good people. It is hard to picture St. Olaf without this exact community of people - with both the diversity and shared values that everyone contributes to the table.

The moral of the story is, “feeling overwhelmed” tells me how fortunate I am to be in this busy and chaotic yet supportive environment. There is always something happening - whether you are interested or not, it is your choice. There is always a place to be - whether this is the kind of student lifestyle you aim for, it is your choice. There is always someone to go to - whether you realise it or not, it is your choice. Now, the last one is indescribably tricky - and as an international freshman myself, I am still working towards reaching its highest potential.

I am not there yet, but I am slowly realising how the game is played. The truth is, and it is kind of paradoxical, you ought not to keep exposing yourself to a variety of options because that is not how life works. Sometimes, you have to limit the options you can choose while you can - before the real world decides it for you. What I am trying to say here is: challenge and experiment with yourself. We may think the fact that we have so many options to choose - is overwhelming. “Wow! I have a variety of options to choose from - I can’t choose!” This is exactly what we take for granted! If you keep picking options and constantly tailoring your day only for the sense of comfortability and familiarity, will you ever find something new and wonderful? (hint, if you are following my story: what did I find in the dining hall across the river?)

Face and overcome the overwhelming inherent nature of any instances you are exposed to. Limited options, chaos, disorder, rush hours - these will lead you to quick decision-making. Fight the overwhelming feeling with sharp decisiveness. This overwhelming feeling, therefore, should not be something that stops or discourages you. What is it then? I am also still exploring it - of course I have not fully mastered it yet. I do have a belief that it is a sign that you are experiencing something new; you are outside your comfort zone, and isn’t that enough to be grateful for? For I argue that many people wish they could shift away from beyond their box. Trust me on that. The next time you feel overwhelmed, step in and face it; you shall find wonders inside, and you might surprise yourself (hint: huge windows and tall ceilings!). Just like my love-hate relationship with Stav, no matter how hard I think about it, and I do not know about you, but: I do not want an ordinary, boring looking dining hall that looks just like a common food court I could easily find back home. Surprise me with chaotic experiences (and beautiful windows and ceilings)! Oh, did I mention that crazy time when the XC table clapped on me because someone dropped their glass from the second floor exactly when I was going downstairs? Anyway -

St. Olaf is reminding me that the world keeps turning, and it is a good thing and I am in the right place.

Written by an international Ole, Class of 2028. She’s a shy, secret hype fan of St. Olaf that would not confess - do not tell anyone.

@ Carleton College, jokes aside, I do love the fact that you have pesto, though, which I ought to try the next time I come there :)
