Let’s Reimagine Democracy Globally

An open letter to my future self in 2045
democracy
reflection
opinions
Author

Cynthia Budiyanto

Published

April 27, 2026

You most likely do not remember this exact feeling, or you may think this younger version of yourself is just being dramatic, but attending both the Introduction to Public Policy course and the Imagining Democracy Globally seminar class was definitely overwhelming. You never thought about the possibility of learning politics and exploring an abundance of ideas and opinions freely, expressively. Your current self was struggling to find her voice and reflect on this matter, as she has never engaged in any political discourse meaningfully, and discussions surrounding politics and its ideas were “better kept personal”.

In case you are wondering, it all began with the simple yet complex question of “Who decides and how?”. Thank her for courageously taking these unfamiliar and uncomfortable courses as it became a stepping stone in learning the term of “effective citizenship”. While your public policy introduction course offers a definite answer to the initial question and you learnt all of the complexities and the intricacies behind the mechanics policymakers are grappling with, your democracy course provides a different perspective by approaching political ideas beyond mere governance structures. The course took you profoundly far from your original question by imagining what is outside the conventional boundaries of our current political system - beyond mere policy debates and elections - and considering the wider purpose of why we are following this pattern of governance in the first place. A new question proliferates - with a heavy consideration of the digital age - on what could democracy possibly look like when we emphasise the fact that it is a “practice”, an ongoing project that requires the engagement and commitment of all individuals, rather than labeling it as a set of institutions or formal processes.

The word democracy used to be a jargon that you never fully understood. However, you started to understand its values and meaning through the course materials. Some of your favourite materials include the early TEDTalks from Brett Hennig, asking what happens if we replace politicians with random people and Shari Davis’s explanation on Participatory Budgeting. Both criticised how our democracy is broken and does not function the way it should, as nowadays, elections resemble merely aristocratic devices. They proposed different kinds of solutions but similar conclusions to this problem: increasing ordinary citizens’ participation in the governing body while emphasising demographic profile diversity. I vividly remember how this strangely coincided with some number of people randomly coming up to me, mentioning that they found out Indonesia was the third largest democracy in the world and “how cool that is”. Considering these course materials, particularly Schmitter and Karl’s “What Democracy Is… and Is Not”, I wondered why this fancy title is just a complete fallacy into what S&K described as “electoralism”, where the mere existence of elections is adequate for democracy. For sure, Indonesia has a great number of electorates and the 82.39% voter turnout ranks Indonesia well above the global average of 65.6% (Westminster Foundation for Democracy 2024), but while I would gladly remind you how democracy severely backslides in the 2024 election (I think you know better how Prabowo’s administration unfolded at the end), I want to discuss a more persistent issue drawn from the intersection of digital era and weak citizenship that does not reflect our voting turnout.

Right now, I can discern how the presence of technology and social media dangerously eats away at citizenship and democratic institutions. While I agree that modern media acts as a catalyst and tool to encourage individuals to articulate their opinions, participate in the public sphere, and challenge state and traditional narratives by the expansion of the public sphere, you must remember what Nanjala Nyabola critically mentioned in her book Digital Democracy, Analogue Politics. She explained how social media platforms we are using were not developed with partly free countries such as Kenya in mind, therefore, we should be wise and careful in implementing its practice into local contexts. Likewise, these technologies were never made for the sake of “digital commons”, let alone to promote social welfare or empower civic participation. Ironically, these companies have profit motives, business models, data-gathering practices, process or procedural opacity and power that do not in any way promote democracy. In the latest election campaign, I witnessed a myriad of buzzers from representatives of political parties or supporters of candidates using Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, WhatsApp, YouTube, and similar applications to exert influence. These buzzers push certain agendas and determine the major campaign narratives. Artificial intelligence and algorithms decide what information is presented to the public through filtering mechanisms where opinions can continue to polarise and radicalise - and no one could be held accountable. There is still a big tendency amongst Indonesians to vote for candidates because of certain race, religion, ethnicity, and familiarity. Voter turn out is high, but can it actually be referred to as “effective citizenship”?

Eventually going and giving back to the community that raised who I am today has been a firm goal that keeps me motivated today. Wherever you are right now in 2045, this year, you are celebrating Indonesia’s 100th year of independence. Whether you would have moved back to Indonesia at this point, this is the time testament where I wish to see a free and independent public sphere where Indonesians have a robust structured discourse within civil society that highly esteemed effective rational-critical debate, therefore resulting in effective resolution of political disputes and genuine democratic engagement. I learned the hard way that technology will inevitably keep developing to become more sophisticated than ever. Whether you like it or not, it is indeed a paradox of development that you would constantly grapple with whatever field you are working in right now.

On the trajectory towards 2045, I argue that resistance against big social media platforms must emerge now. These corporations only allow people to receive comfortable information and reinforce existing tendencies toward confirmation bias. With Indonesia’s persistent problem regarding literacy, I worry that my aspiration is only wishful thinking which means that by 2045, despite celebrating the country’s milestone, its citizens will continue to be dictated and controlled with instant and most readily available information in online platforms, further reinforcing a general public who is uninterested in being educated and understanding opposing viewpoints.

While I, realistically, do not foresee you being a policy-maker passing bills in the matter of transforming the Indonesian education curriculum, specifically establishing a civic education that goes beyond strengthening national identity but also penetrating democracy values, you ought to remember one thing: that your younger self has long been passionate about the transforming impact of education. I hope that amidst all of your ambitious science-related endeavours, you would spare a meaningful amount of time and energy to contribute to Indonesia’s education sector in any possible way. You have realised for a long time how democracy can only work if there is an informed citizenry. Advocating for technological literacy or being involved in research to provide policy recommendations could be some ways to fight against artificial intelligence and algorithm infestation. Over the years, I believe that you, by now, will have shaped a confident and firm belief in media literacy and civic responsibility and why they matter for democracy, which will naturally guide your way into advocacy or policy research.

At the end, politics was never supposed to be something personal. Calhoun, through his book “Civil Society and the Public Sphere”, mentioned that being a part of the civil society means coordinating our common affairs together through collective discourse, which transcends the private interests of each other. We must debate, share our piece of cultural creativity and diversity to benefit the larger public concern dialogue. One crucial key is therefore courage. Deeply inspired by Wangari Maathai’s work through the Green Belt movement, she fundamentally educated Kenyan women in particular regarding their rights to speak up and how to stand up for what they believe in despite intimidation.

Every human being deserves to amplify their voices and opinions. It is our responsibility to guide the future generation into meaningfully participating in public discourse. The standard of the common idea of democracy has to be raised. We should present what democracy could look like, rather than what it is. Democracy can only happen when people find their voices, opinions, and values respected and heard. Democracy does not happen just because “people participate”, which, most of the time, is just limited to elections and voting. Democracy on a personal level happens when everyone’s mind is free and has the capability to see the truth. Democracy happens when people stand up for things they believe in. This also means that real democracy can only happen when there are efforts to advance the thinking capability of a nation, encouraging people to realise what their rights are. To merely participate in democracy is easier than actively engaging in its deeper responsibilities: understanding the complexities of various issues, mobilising and empowering communities, and, importantly, holding leaders accountable. Actions toward effective citizenship demand sustained effort. You must light and pass that torch of courage and determination in order for this to happen.
